Project Overview

Abstract

The Multi-Agent Robotics Project (Nicknamed GoScout) aims to develop a system of two autonomous agents capable of working together to survey and interact with an environment. In effect, this will consist of an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) and a ground-based robotic rover. The UAV is able to quickly scan a specified area for points of interest and relay GPS information to the rover. The rover is then to navigate to the provided location. These criteria require autonomous operation, relative location sharing, and robust communication.

Mission objectives

The purpose of the MultiAgent system is to scout a large environment for a specific target using a UAV, then navigate to the target with the rover. Currently, the target is a large square that sits on the floor, identifiable either by a large red square, or a QR code. The drone will autonomously fly in a search pattern until the camera identifies the objective. Once the objective is found, the location is shared with the rover in the form of GPS coordinates sent across the wireless network. At this point, the Rover navigates to the target using an onboard GPS along with other sensors.

The Rover

Our rover builds off JPL’s Open Source Rover Project. The rover uses Rocker-Bogie Suspension and 6-Wheel Ackerman Steering. Thanks to previous student projects, much of the chassis is already designed, including suspension arms, a steering system, and attachment points for a robotic arm and solar recharging system. The Rover is controlled by a Raspberry Pi that executes our python code for autonomous operation. The Pi is connected to 5 separate motor controllers via separate UART channels for redundancy.

The Drone

We’re using a quadcopter drone for its maneuverability and stability. Much of the UAV development was completed by previous students. A Raspberry Pi controls high-level commands and communicates with the flight controller as well as the rover. The UAV uses a PixHawk flight controller running Ardupilot to maintain stable flight. Sensors include a GPS, IMU, and global shutter camera.

Communication

Our team is working to design a robust communication protocol between the two agents as well as additional agents or ground stations that could be introduced in the future. Currently, the rover hosts a wireless network that other devices can connect to. Our custom protocol is TCP based, and uses a flexible packet structure to allow for many types of commands and information sharing. Additionally, we are working with a Cal Poly grad student to implement an AdHoc network such that multiple agents could communicate across a mesh network.

General Progress

Currently, we are nearing completion in Rover Assembly. We are manufacturing the final mechanical components and are almost done with the electrical systems. We’ve written the basic software for controlling motors including speed control and steering calibration.

We’re working on integrating a new camera with the Drone in order to counteract visual artifacts caused by Flight Vibrations.

As far as communication goes, we’re able to open a socket between devices. At this point, we’re developing the new protocol, focusing on the basic packet structure.

Future Development and Considerations

To fully utilize the benefits of a mesh network and enhance mission variability, I’d like to introduce a third vehicle. This agent may be ground or air-based and fulfill a role that neither the UAV nor Rover are capable of. Currently, I have two ideas for what this agent would entail.

Fast Light Ground Vehicle

Fixed Wing High Altitude Vehicle

Our current rover is capable and well-equipped, yet limited in speed. The introduction of another, faster ground vehicle would allow for expedited exploration. As the UAV finds points of interest, the new agent could rapidly drive to them and collect basic information. This light vehicle would be limited in capabilities, sensors, etc to save weight. The current rover would serve as a base station for the smaller rover, perhaps providing a place to recharge or collecting the lighter vehicle to carry over more difficult terrain. Additionally, several light ground vehicles could work collaboratively, using their multitude to quickly survey an area or triangulate positions.

While the current UAV is nimble, it's limited in flight duration. The introduction of a fixed-wing vehicle would provide a high-altitude view. A high-gain antenna would allow the new vehicle to act as a communication relay bridging the network between the rover and other network-attached devices (ground stations, drone, etc). This would allow agents to travel to different areas and maintain a connection to pass information. The vehicle would host a limited selection of sensors and hardware to reduce weight. A gimbaled camera and lidar system would be instrumental in providing topographical data to other vehicles.

Universal Integration

It would be great to create a flexible, highly capable system of integrating different robots and control centers. The novel communication protocol is a step in the right direction as it creates a uniform way to send information and commands between devices. Moving forward, it could be advantageous to design a flexible electronic control board that could be deployed on different vehicles. Such a device would likely consist of a control computer as well as a power management system and have additional expansion capabilities to interface with motors, sensors, etc on different vehicles.
